## Project Overview: Evidence-Based Workforce Diagnostics

This portfolio contains five specialized analytics projects focused on Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI), HR metrics, and organizational psychology. Each project applies rigorous data analysis to real-world datasets to uncover systemic patterns influencing workforce behavior, providing actionable insights for HR professionals and organizational leaders.

Drawing from conservation of resources theory (Hobfoll, 1989) and job embeddedness theory (Mitchell et al., 2001), this work treats workforce metrics not as isolated incidents but as symptoms of broader organizational health requiring evidence-based intervention strategies.

---

## Analytical Framework: Organizational Psychology Lens

**What the data shows:** Systematic collection and analysis of HR metrics reveals patterns in attrition, compensation, representation, and mental health that transcend surface-level reporting.

**Psychologist's Take:** Through the lens of organizational psychology, workforce analytics becomes organizational diagnosis. As Hobfoll (1989) established, individuals strive to obtain, retain, protect, and foster valued resources — when work fails to provide sufficient resources for recovery, disengagement occurs regardless of satisfaction levels. This perspective shifts focus from descriptive HR reporting to identifying systemic friction points.

**Business Meaning:** By applying theoretical frameworks to empirical data, organizations can move beyond reactive incident response to evidence-based risk stratification, enabling HR to function as a strategic partner answering "who is at risk, and what does intervention cost?"

## Project Highlight: IBM HR Employee Attrition Analysis

**What the data shows:** Analysis of 1,470 employee records reveals 16.1% overall attrition rate, with employees reporting frequent overtime showing 30.5% attrition rate (2.9x higher than non-overtime employees at 10.4%), and those with <1 year tenure showing 36.4% attrition (3.4x higher than >5 year tenure employees at 10.8%).

**Psychologist's Take:** The 2.9x higher attrition rate among employees working frequent overtime reveals workload as a critical driver of turnover operating independently of compensation. As Bakker, Demerouti, and Sanz-Vergel (2014) note in their Job Demands-Resources approach, excessive job demands lead to energy depletion and health problems when not balanced by sufficient job resources. This aligns with conservation of resources theory, which posits that individuals strive to obtain, retain, protect, and foster valued resources — when work fails to provide sufficient resources for recovery, disengagement occurs regardless of satisfaction levels.

**Business Meaning:** Addressing overtime requires structural interventions that reduce excessive demands while providing adequate recovery mechanisms, rather than merely compensatory approaches. Targeted interventions for high-risk groups prevent disproportionate replacement costs.

## Project Highlight: DEI Dashboard EMEA - Representation & Pay Equity

**What the data shows:** Analysis of 10,000 employee records reveals 33.9% female representation with 20.4% female attrition rate (1.33x higher than male attrition rate of 15.3%), 7.9% gender pay gap favoring males, and -0.6 pp female representation change from 2020-2023.

**Psychologist's Take:** The disproportionate attrition rate among female employees despite substantial workforce representation suggests systemic barriers to retention transcending simple headcount metrics. As Ely (1995) found, the persistence of gender differences in advancement and retention indicates organizational practices, not individual characteristics, drive disparities. The 1.33 attrition ratio indicates that for every male employee who leaves, 1.33 female employees exit — a disparity that compounds representation gaps over time.

**Business Meaning:** The concentration of male employees in two-thirds of the workforce creates structural imbalances influencing organizational culture and decision-making. Critical mass theory suggests that until women constitute at least 30% of decision-making bodies, their ability to influence outcomes remains limited, making representation analysis within functional units essential.

## Project Highlight: OSMI Mental Health in Tech Analytics

**What the data shows:** Analysis of 1,250 technology professionals reveals 40.9% comfortable discussing mental health with supervisors, 61% fearing career consequences, and 44.9% unable to predict mental health leave ease, with supervisor trust showing negative correlation with work interference (r = -0.18).

**Psychologist's Take:** The finding that only 40.9% of employees feel comfortable discussing mental health with supervisors, while 61% fear career consequences, reveals supervisory relationships as the critical gateway to psychological safety in organizations. As Edmondson (1999) demonstrated, psychological safety and learning behavior in work teams shows managerial behavior as the primary mediator between organizational policy and individual experience. Supervisors control access to resources, influence performance evaluations, and shape daily work interactions that either enable or inhibit vulnerability.

**Business Meaning:** Supervisory trust functions as a critical gateway resource for accessing organizational mental health support. By building supervisor capacity for psychological safety, organizations transform managers from potential sources of risk into conduits of support, aligning with social learning theory's emphasis on modeled behaviors creating organizational norms.

## Cross-Cutting Insights: The Structural Diagnostic Approach

**What the data shows:** Across projects, recurring insights emerge: attrition driven by workload and psychological factors rather than compensation alone, significant disparities in lived experiences despite near-parity representation, and supervisory relationships as key leverage points for organizational change.

**Psychologist's Take:** The consistent finding that "attrition is rarely about pay alone" reflects a deeper organizational truth — employees stay or leave based on complex psychological contracts and resource dynamics. As Rousseau (1989) established, the psychological contract — the unwritten set of expectations between employee and employer — is the invisible architecture of the employment relationship. When it fractures, no compensation adjustment will rebuild it.

**Business Meaning:** Organizations seeking to improve workforce outcomes must move beyond symptomatic treatment to address structural factors. This diagnostic approach enables precise resource allocation to high-risk areas rather than blanket interventions, creating sustainable improvements in retention, engagement, and performance.

## Strategic Intervention Frameworks

**OJT Framework** (IBM HR Analysis): Overtime workload management, Job satisfaction enhancement, Enhanced onboarding and early support

**R.E.P. Framework** (DEI Dashboard EMEA): Representation equity, Equity in compensation, Progression equity

**S.A.F.E. Framework** (OSMI Mental Health): Supervisor focus, Access transparency, Fear reduction, Equity for remote workers

**What the data shows:** These evidence-based frameworks structure intervention recommendations around key diagnostic findings, creating predictable, actionable pathways from insight to implementation.

**Psychologist's Take:** By naming and structuring interventions around empirically validated patterns, organizations create accountability mechanisms and ensure resources target verified drivers of workforce outcomes rather than assumptions or trends.

**Business Meaning:** Strategic frameworks enable continuous improvement cycles rather than one-time corrections, positioning HR as a strategic partner capable of identifying and addressing systemic barriers impacting organizational performance.

## Business Impact & Value Proposition

* **Cost Avoidance:** Replacing a mid-level employee costs approximately 1.5× their annual salary (SHRM, 2022). With identified high-risk groups (overtime workers, low tenure employees, those fearing disclosure), targeted interventions prevent disproportionate replacement costs.
* **Productivity Protection:** Addressing turnover drivers preserves organizational knowledge and expertise that would be lost through turnover and replacement cycles, maintaining productivity levels and organizational effectiveness.
* **Strategic Credibility:** Demonstrating a shift from reactive incident response to evidence-based risk stratification enables HR to function as a strategic partner by answering "who is at risk, and what does intervention cost?" rather than merely reporting utilization statistics.
* **Talent Optimization:** Moving beyond representational counts to examine progression, compensation, and experience metrics demonstrates a shift from compliance-focused DEI to evidence-based talent optimization.

## Future Scope: The Next Phase

* **Predictive Flight Risk Modeling:** Logistic Regression or Random Forest classifiers trained on overtime patterns, job satisfaction scores, tenure, and other HR metrics would produce individual-level "Probability of Exit" scores enabling pre-emptive retention conversations.
* **Multi-Level Modeling:** Separating individual, supervisory, and organizational effects on workforce outcomes would identify precise leverage points for intervention and quantify variance explained at each level.
* **Longitudinal Concealment Gap Tracking:** Measuring the difference between mental health symptom prevalence and actual help-seeking rates pre/post organizational interventions would quantify structural change impact.
* **Qualitative Supervisory Behavior Analysis:** In-depth exploration of specific supervisory behaviors that build or erode trust in mental health disclosures would provide actionable training content beyond general psychological safety principles.
* **Intersectional Analysis:** Applying intersectionality theory to examine how overlapping identities create unique experiences of inclusion or exclusion that single-axis analyses cannot capture.
