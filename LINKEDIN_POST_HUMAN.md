Just spent some time updating my people analytics portfolio - figured it was about time to showcase what I've been working on.

Been diving into HR metrics lately, trying to look beyond the usual turnover reports and engagement scores to really get at what's happening with people at work. You know how it is - we collect all this data but sometimes miss the forest for the trees.

A few things that stood out:

First, looked at that IBM HR dataset - turns out people working lots of overtime are leaving at like 30.5%, which is nearly 3x higher than those with normal hours. Wild, right? Makes you wonder if we're just burning people out instead of fixing the workload.

Then there was the DEI stuff for EMEA - 33.9% women overall sounds decent until you see they're leaving at 20.4% versus 15.3% for men. So for every guy who walks out, 1.33 women are doing the same. That gap adds up fast over time.

And the mental health tech survey from OSMI - only about 4 in 10 feel safe telling their boss they're struggling. Over 60% are worried it'll tank their career. Supervisors are basically the gatekeepers here, whether we like it or not.

What's interesting is how these aren't just random HR problems - they're symptoms of deeper stuff. When work keeps draining people's energy without giving enough back to recover (shoutout to Hobfoll's old conservation of resources idea), people check out. It's not always about the paycheck - it's about whether the job feels sustainable, whether you trust your boss, whether you feel like you belong.

That's why I've been playing with these little frameworks - OJT for the overtime stuff (managing workload, boosting joy, better onboarding) and S.A.F.E. for the mental health side (focus on bosses, make access easy, kill the fear, remote equity). Gives us a way to actually target fixes instead of spraying and praying.

Honestly, mixing the Python/Power BI side with the org psych lens has been surprisingly useful. Lets us move from "here's what happened" to "here's why it matters and what we might actually do about it."
