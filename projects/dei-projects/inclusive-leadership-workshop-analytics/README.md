## Project Overview: Diagnosing Workforce Dynamics Through HR Metrics

This project analyzes HR employee data to measure the effectiveness of inclusive leadership training programs through leadership competency proxies. Analysis of 3,400 employee records reveals overall leadership competency of 2.97/5.0, with Development & Growth Mindset as the lowest competency (2.60/5.0) and Experience & Role Stability as the highest (3.13/5.0). Gender analysis shows minimal disparities in leadership competencies.

Dataset: HR Employee Dataset (Kaggle) (3,400 employee records)
Tools: Python (Pandas, NumPy, Seaborn, Matplotlib) · Jupyter Notebook
Overall attrition rate: Not applicable (leadership competency analysis)

## Core Organizational Findings

### 1. Leadership Competency Paradox
- **What the data shows:** Overall leadership competency: 2.97/5.0; Experience & Role Stability: 3.13/5.0 (highest); Development & Growth Mindset: 2.60/5.0 (lowest); Development gap: 0.53 points
- **Psychologist's Take:** The significant disparity between highest and lowest leadership competencies reveals a critical development imbalance in the leadership pipeline. As Dweck (2006) established in her mindset research, "the view you adopt for yourself profoundly affects the way you lead your life," indicating that a fixed mindset in growth areas can severely limit leadership effectiveness. The 0.53-point gap between Experience & Role Stability (3.13) and Development & Growth Mindset (2.60) suggests leaders may be relying on tenure and stability rather than continuous learning and adaptability. This creates vulnerability in today's rapidly changing business environment where learning agility is paramount. Organizations must address this imbalance by fostering growth mindsets while leveraging existing strengths in experience and stability.

### 2. Gender Representation Paradox
- **What the data shows:** Gender distribution: 24.6% female, 24.4% male, 25.7% non-binary, 25.3% prefer not to say; Gender competency disparities: Self-Awareness & Engagement (Females 0.04 lower), Decision-Making & Results Orientation (Females 0.06 higher), Psychological Safety & Well-being (Females 0.05 higher), Development & Growth Mindset (Females 0.02 higher), Experience & Role Stability (Females 0.01 higher), Retention & Psychological Safety (Females 0.05 lower)
- **Psychologist's Take:** The near parity in gender representation coupled with minimal and mixed disparities in leadership competencies suggests a relatively equitable leadership development environment across gender groups. As Ibarra (1993) found in her network centrality study, "women are less likely than men to have network ties to individuals in positions of authority," which would typically manifest in competency gaps. The absence of significant disparities indicates that leadership development opportunities may be relatively equitably distributed. The mixed pattern (with females scoring higher in some areas and lower in others) suggests specialization rather than systemic bias. According to Nishii (2013), "the climate for inclusion encompasses employees' perceptions of being valued for their uniqueness while belonging to the organization," and the minimal competency gaps suggest an inclusive climate where diverse leadership styles are valued rather than a single prototypical leadership ideal being enforced.

### 3. Retention Predictor Paradox
- **What the data shows:** Retention & Psychological Safety: 2.97/5.0 (moderate score); Correlation with retention risk: 1.000 (perfect); Development & Growth Mindset: 2.60/5.0 (lowest competency); Experience & Role Stability: 3.13/5.0 (highest competency)
- **Psychologist's Take:** The perfect correlation (1.000) between Retention & Psychological Safety and retention risk reveals a critical insight: psychological safety specifically related to retention is the make-or-break factor in employee retention, regardless of overall leadership competency levels. As Edmondson (1999) established in her psychological safety research, "psychological safety describes a climate in which people feel safe enough to take interpersonal risks by speaking up and sharing concerns, questions, or mistakes." The fact that this specific competency shows perfect predictive power while overall leadership competency is merely moderate (2.97/5.0) indicates that retention hinges on this specific psychological mechanism rather than broad leadership proficiency. This finding aligns with conservation of resources theory (Hobfoll, 1989), which posits that psychological safety represents a critical resource that, when present, enables engagement and retention, but when absent, triggers disengagement regardless of other positive factors.
